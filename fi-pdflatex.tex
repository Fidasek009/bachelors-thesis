The application of molecular dynamics (MD) has evolved significantly since its inception in the 1950s~\cite{md_for_all}. The subsequent rise of computational power has made it highly accessible and practical. Its adoption accelerated during the COVID-19 pandemic, where it played a crucial role in computational drug discovery efforts, particularly in the identification of potential inhibitors~\cite{md_in_covid}.

A primary challenge current MD scientists face is the lack of standardization in setting up, tuning, running, and sharing simulations. The most popular MD simulation software, GROMACS, relies on a command-line interface, meaning users frequently depend on custom scripts and manual configuration. MD simulations are computationally expensive, necessitating the correct selection of hyperparameters, such as the number of MPI processes or the allocation of CPU and GPU resources. Determining these hyperparameters is complex, and sub-optimal configurations lead to significant computational overhead and resource inefficiency. Upon obtaining the results, researchers require separate sets of tools for analysis, further increasing workflow overhead. After completing this complex process, reusing the results is difficult due to the absence of a standardized repository for sharing molecular dynamics data; data exchange consequently relies on custom shell scripts and non-standard methodologies. This environment creates a barrier between teams and hinders collaboration.

MDDash is a tool created to address these challenges. It aims to provide scientists with an efficient workflow that integrates simulation, analysis, and data management. It provides an easy to use web interface for setting up, running and analyzing molecular dynamics simulations using GROMACS and AMBER. It also includes an automatic hyperparameter tuner that optimizes the simulation parameters based on the user's hardware and simulation system. To support reproducibility and data preservation, MDDash utilizes reusable Jupyter notebooks that record all simulation steps, implements automatic engine-specific metadata extraction, and integrates directly with the InvenioRDM repository for publishing simulation results. These features ensure that simulation data is well-documented, standardized, and easily accessible for future use.


\chapter{Molecular Dynamics Landscape}
\label{ch:md_landscape}

Molecular dynamics simulation is a computational technique used to model the physical movements of atoms and molecules over time. It provides insights into the structure, dynamics, and interactions of biological macromolecules, which are crucial for drug discovery and understanding fundamental biological processes~\cite{md_for_all}.

Experimental methods such as X-ray crystallography and nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy can resolve static atomic-level structures. However, the primary benefit of molecular dynamics lies in its ability to capture dynamic behavior and time-resolved processes that are difficult to observe experimentally. These include conformational transitions, transient intermediate states, and rare events.

By enabling the calculation of thermodynamic properties such as binding free energies, molecular dynamics serves as a bridge between theoretical models and experimental observations, providing quantitative insight into processes that neither approach can access alone.

Beyond this scientific role, MD reduces the cost and time associated with traditional wet-lab experiments. By allowing researchers to pre-screen candidates and simulate complex biological environments computationally, it narrows the experimental search space before committing to expensive laboratory validation.

However, setting up and running such simulations is a complex process that involves multiple steps, each with its own set of parameters and configurations. This complexity can be a barrier for researchers, especially those who are new to the field or do not have extensive computational expertise.

\section{Typical Molecular Dynamics Workflow}
\label{sec:md_workflow}

When conducting MD research, scientists typically follow this workflow~\cite{gmx_workflow}:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{System preparation}: obtaining or building an initial structure, generating a force field-compliant topology, defining the simulation box, adding solvent molecules, and neutralizing the system with ions.
    \item \textbf{Energy minimization}: relaxing the system to remove steric clashes and inappropriate geometry introduced during setup.
    \item \textbf{Equilibration}: gradually bringing the system to target temperature and pressure, typically in two phases --- first under the NVT ensemble (constant number of particles, volume, and temperature), then under the NPT ensemble (constant number of particles, pressure, and temperature).
    \item \textbf{Production MD}: running the simulation with restraints released to collect trajectory data for analysis.
    \item \textbf{Analysis}: post-processing trajectories to extract properties of interest, such as structural stability, energetics, and dynamic behavior.
\end{enumerate}

Notably absent from this standard workflow is any systematic step for selecting computational performance parameters, such as the balance between MPI ranks and OpenMP threads or GPU assignment. In practice, researchers typically rely on prior experience or institutional documentation to configure these settings, as no general automated benchmarking tool exists that covers the full parameter space for a given system and hardware combination. This often results in suboptimal configurations and unnecessary waste of computational resources.

\section{Molecular Dynamics Simulation Software}
\label{sec:md_software}

For running molecular dynamics simulations, several software packages are available, each with its own strengths and weaknesses. A comparative study by Shirts et al.~\cite{gmx_software_comparison} examines a representative set of these engines: GROMACS~\cite{gromacs}, AMBER~\cite{amber}, LAMMPS~\cite{lammps}, CHARMM~\cite{charmm}, and DESMOND~\cite{desmond}. While these programs are designed to perform similar tasks and evaluate the same underlying physical models, they often have different default parameters, integration schemes, and even variations in fundamental constants (such as Coulomb's constant). These differences can lead to variations in simulation results and make cross-engine reproducibility challenging if not carefully managed~\cite{gmx_software_comparison}.

Among the available engines, GROMACS is one of the most established and widely used, owing to its high performance, extensive feature set, and active development community. AMBER is another prominent choice, particularly valued for its well-validated force fields and GPU-accelerated simulation support.

Despite its widespread adoption, GROMACS is primarily designed as a command-line tool, which can make it less accessible to users unfamiliar with terminal-based workflows. This often leads to reliance on custom scripts and manual setup processes, which can be error-prone and difficult to reproduce.

\section{Reproducibility and Data Management}
\label{sec:reproducibility}

The \enquote{reproducibility crisis} in computational molecular science stems from a confluence of methodological factors. Differences in software versions and compilation environments, undocumented parameter choices, force field inconsistencies, and platform-dependent numerical behavior can all prevent independent verification of published results~\cite{md_reproducibility}. Addressing these methodological issues requires standardized reporting practices and transparent documentation of simulation parameters, which remain an open challenge in the field.

A separate but compounding problem is the lack of standardized infrastructure for sharing simulation data. Modern MD simulations generate massive volumes of trajectory data, creating a \enquote{data deluge}. Historically, these outputs were stored on local hard drives or institutional servers, often discarded after publication. Driven by Open Science mandates and the FAIR (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable) principles, there is now a strong demand for robust data stewardship that addresses this infrastructural dimension of the problem~\cite{md_reproducibility}.

In the absence of a unified global infrastructure, researchers frequently rely on generalist data repositories such as Zenodo~\cite{zenodo}, Figshare~\cite{figshare} and Open Science Framework (OSF)~\cite{osf}. Zenodo, in particular, is widely used and is built upon the InvenioRDM framework~\cite{InvenioRDM} -- a turn-key research data management platform developed by CERN. InvenioRDM is designed to handle large-scale research data, providing long-term preservation and assigning persistent identifiers like Digital Object Identifiers (DOIs).

While generalist repositories provide essential infrastructure, their agnostic nature allows the deposition of arbitrary file types without enforcing domain-specific metadata standards. This practice has led to the accumulation of \enquote{dark matter}~\cite{md_repo_problem} -- petabytes of trajectory files that lack the necessary contextual metadata (such as force field parameters, integration temperatures, or system topologies) required for independent verification or automated meta-analysis.

To address the limitations of generalist platforms, the biomolecular community has developed domain-specific repositories, such as GPCRmd~\cite{GPCRmd} or MoDEL~\cite{MoDEL}. These specialized platforms enforce strict metadata schemas and integrate web-based visualization tools. However, this specialization introduces architectural fragmentation, as independent databases often utilize disparate ontologies and APIs, hindering cross-domain interoperability.

Bridging the gap between robust generalist infrastructure and domain-specific requirements is essential for the future of MD data sharing. Platforms like MDRepo~\cite{mdrepo_ui} address this architectural challenge. MDRepo is built on top of the same InvenioRDM framework that powers Zenodo, inheriting its scalable architecture and persistent identifier management. However, unlike Zenodo, MDRepo is explicitly tailored for molecular dynamics, enforcing rigorous metadata schemas and standardizing trajectory formats. By integrating such a repository directly into the simulation workflow, researchers can ensure that their data is not only preserved but remains fully interpretable and reusable for future computational research.

\section{Analysis Tools for Molecular Dynamics Simulations}
\label{sec:md_analysis_tools}

The analysis stage is increasingly limited by data handling rather than by the computational cost of individual metrics. Contemporary trajectories can reach terabyte scale, and traditional post-processing based on ad hoc shell scripts and local desktop tools often provides insufficient provenance, weak metadata consistency, and limited reuse across projects. In current practice, this problem is addressed by combining tools for standardized trajectory curation with tools for interactive inspection~\cite{md_analysis_tools}.

The MDDB Workflow~\cite{mddb_workflow} is one such curation tool. In the literature, it is described as an engine-agnostic workflow tool for harmonizing datasets produced by major simulation packages while preserving provenance and metadata required for independent reuse. Compared with manually assembled analysis scripts, this tool reduces dataset heterogeneity and improves cross-study comparability.

For interactive analysis and communication of results, Mol*~\cite{molstar_viewer} is a widely used visualization tool. In contrast to desktop-oriented tools such as VMD~\cite{VMD}, PyMOL~\cite{PyMOL}, and ChimeraX~\cite{ChimeraX}, Mol* is described as better suited to collaborative inspection of large structural datasets with low setup overhead for users. Relative to earlier web visualization tools such as NGL~\cite{ngl_viewer}, the reported advantages are improved scalability and a more suitable basis for sharing trajectory-derived insights.

Taken together, MDDB Workflow and Mol* illustrate two complementary state-of-the-art tool directions in molecular simulation analysis: standardization of data interpretation and improved accessibility of trajectory inspection. This differs from traditional script-centric practice, where reproducibility and interoperability are often addressed only after analysis has been completed. The shift is consistent with broader FAIR-driven recommendations in the field~\cite{mddb_fair}.

\section{Virtual Research Environments}
\label{sec:VREs}

To address the complexities of MD orchestration and data management, the scientific community has developed Virtual Research Environments (VREs). These comprehensive, web-based platforms provide researchers with secure access to distributed HPC tools, federated data repositories, and collaborative services. A prominent example is the BioExcel Center of Excellence and its BioExcel Building Blocks (BioBB) library~\cite{BioBB}, which encapsulates complex biomolecular simulation tools into interoperable Python wrappers distributed as Jupyter Notebooks~\cite{jupyter} -- allowing researchers to execute, document, and share entire simulation pipelines within a single readable document.

Despite these advancements, several challenges remain. Modern MD simulations routinely produce trajectories that exceed the storage and transfer capacities of individual workstations~\cite{hpc_approaches_for_md}, requiring centralized infrastructure where analytical tools operate directly alongside the data. While tools like BioBB lower the barrier to entry through Jupyter Notebooks, many existing VREs still require significant expertise in command-line interfaces, complex scripting, and manual infrastructure management, maintaining a steep learning curve for experimental researchers.

\section{Identified Gaps}
\label{sec:identified_gaps}

The preceding sections reveal four distinct limitations of the current MD simulation landscape that collectively motivate the platform designed in this thesis.

The standard MD workflow (Section~\ref{sec:md_workflow}) contains no systematic step for selecting computational performance parameters. Researchers are therefore left to rely on prior experience or institutional documentation, which frequently results in suboptimal hardware utilization.

The principal simulation tools, including GROMACS and AMBER (Section~\ref{sec:md_software}), expose their functionality exclusively through command-line interfaces. This design requires users to manage complex parameter files and custom scripts manually, which raises the entry barrier and increases the risk of undocumented or non-reproducible configurations.

Despite progress in both generalist and domain-specific repositories, data stewardship remains fragmented (Section~\ref{sec:reproducibility}). Generalist repositories lack enforced domain-specific metadata schemas, while specialized platforms rely on disparate ontologies that limit cross-study interoperability.

The analysis stage is not integrated with the rest of the simulation lifecycle (Sections~\ref{sec:md_analysis_tools} and~\ref{sec:VREs}). Existing VREs reduce certain barriers through tools such as Jupyter notebooks, but they do not unify the full pipeline --- from initial setup through data publication --- under a single interface with consistent provenance tracking.

These limitations affect the same researcher within a single workflow. An integrated platform that systematically addresses the entry barrier, automates performance tuning, enforces reproducible data stewardship, and connects analysis with the broader simulation lifecycle is therefore the subject of the following chapter.


\chapter{MDDash Design and Requirements}
\label{ch:mddash_design}

MDDash was designed as an integrated platform that guides users through the complete simulation lifecycle --- from initial structure preparation through publication to a domain-specific repository --- under a unified interface. Its design directly addresses the four gaps identified in Section~\ref{sec:identified_gaps}.

\section{Platform choice}

To focus on integrating the workflow rather than building application infrastructure from scratch, JupyterHub~\cite{jupyter} was chosen as the foundation for user management and resource allocation. As discussed in Section~\ref{sec:VREs}, researchers are already familiar with the Jupyter ecosystem, and most computational biochemists work regularly with Python and its scientific libraries. Using Jupyter notebooks as the main interface for simulation setup and analysis was therefore a natural fit, minimizing the learning overhead for the target user base.

JupyterHub also provides the technical capabilities required by a multi-user platform. Each user receives an isolated workspace, which is essential for running multiple computations concurrently without interference. Additionally, JupyterHub deploys readily on Kubernetes, enabling robust resource management and horizontal scalability as demand grows.

\section{Simulation engine}

As discussed in Section~\ref{sec:md_software}, several MD engines are available, each with distinct strengths. MDDash supports GROMACS and AMBER as its simulation engines. GROMACS is the primary engine, as it is the most widely adopted in the biomolecular simulation community and is the tool of choice for the target users. AMBER is supported as an alternative for users whose workflows depend on its force field ecosystem.

To accommodate multiple engines, all engine-specific logic is encapsulated in discrete modules within the Dashboard API and the MDRun API. Adding support for a new engine therefore requires only the implementation of the corresponding module, with no changes to the surrounding workflow logic, user interface, or infrastructure.

\section{Workflow design}

Following the workflow structure described in Section~\ref{sec:md_workflow}, MDDash was designed to guide users through all necessary steps of their molecular dynamics simulations. The workflow is structured as a linear wizard with six distinct steps:

\begin{enumerate}
    \setcounter{enumi}{-1}
    \item \textbf{New experiment}
    \item \textbf{Setup}
    \item \textbf{Tune}
    \item \textbf{Run}
    \item \textbf{Analyze}
    \item \textbf{Publish}
\end{enumerate}

These steps are designed to provide a clear and structured path for users, ensuring that they do not miss any critical components of the simulation process. By breaking down the workflow into discrete stages, MDDash promotes reproducibility by enforcing a standardized sequence of actions and capturing all necessary metadata at each step. This contrasts with the traditional unstructured terminal approach, where users may skip steps or fail to document their process, leading to difficulties in reproducing results.

\section{Workflow steps}
\label{sec:workflow_steps}

Each step in the MDDash workflow is designed to address specific challenges in the MD simulation process, with a focus on automation, user-friendliness, and reproducibility.

\subsection{New experiment}
\label{sec:step_new_experiment}

The initialization of a new experiment is designed with a focus on reproducibility and workflow flexibility. To facilitate these principles, MDDash supports several methods for retrieving starting structures and simulation parameters. Users can instantiate an experiment directly from the MDRepo repository, enabling the reuse of verified, previously published simulations as templates. Alternatively, structural data can be fetched by providing a PDB ID from the RCSB Protein Data Bank (PDB) or by uploading local configuration files directly.

Furthermore, the environment allows users to specify a remote Git repository for the Jupyter notebooks used in subsequent setup and analysis stages. This feature includes support for Binder-compatible repositories, which provides native compatibility with the BioExcel Building Blocks (BioBB) library discussed in Section~\ref{sec:VREs}. By integrating these diverse input sources, the platform ensures that researchers adhere to standardized protocols and also have the flexibility to customize their workflows according to their specific research needs.

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:step_setup}

Traditionally, setting up MD simulations involves a series of manual commands executed in a terminal, often documented in a text file or a custom script. This approach can lead to issues with reproducibility, as it relies heavily on the user's diligence in recording every step and parameter used. In contrast, using reusable Jupyter notebooks for the setup process offers several advantages:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Documentation and Reproducibility}: Jupyter notebooks inherently combine code, documentation, and results in a single document. This allows researchers to capture the entire setup process, including the rationale behind each step, in a format that is easy to understand and reproduce.
    \item \textbf{Flexibility and Customization}: Notebooks can be easily modified and extended. Researchers can add new cells for additional setup steps, modify existing code to accommodate specific requirements, or integrate new tools without needing to rewrite an entire script.
    \item \textbf{Interactive Environment}: Jupyter notebooks provide an interactive environment where researchers can execute code cells individually, allowing for iterative development and immediate feedback. This is particularly beneficial during the setup phase, where adjustments may be necessary based on intermediate results.
    \item \textbf{Integration with BioBB}: By supporting Binder-compatible repositories, the platform allows users to leverage the BioExcel Building Blocks library, which provides pre-built workflows for biomolecular simulations. This integration further enhances the ease of use and standardization of the setup process.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Tune}
\label{sec:step_tune}

Correct parameter selection for the simulation is often omitted from the traditional MD workflow~(Section~\ref{sec:md_workflow}). It is, however, the most important step for efficient use of computational resources, as incorrect settings can cause the simulation to run significantly slower than necessary, resulting in increased costs and longer time to results. The tune step in MDDash addresses this gap through integration with the GROMACS Tuner~\cite{gromacs_tuner} service, which performs short benchmark runs across different configurations and selects the best one based on performance metrics. By automating this process, MDDash ensures that users achieve optimal performance without needing to manually experiment with different settings.

\subsection{Run}
\label{sec:step_run}

After obtaining the optimal parameters from the tuning step, users can proceed to run their simulations directly from the MDDash interface. The platform utilizes Kubernetes Jobs to manage the execution of simulations, ensuring correct resource allocation and isolation for each simulation run. The MDRun API~(Section~\ref{sec:mdrun_api}) serves as the interface through which MDDash starts and submits these jobs.

Once a simulation is running, MDDash provides continuous observability into its progress. Users can track resource usage and job status in real-time through the MDRun API, and live file access allows inspection of intermediate output files without waiting for the simulation to complete. This enables researchers to detect problems early and make informed decisions about whether to continue or abort a run.

\subsection{Analyze}
\label{sec:step_analyze}

As discussed in Sections~\ref{sec:reproducibility} and~\ref{sec:md_analysis_tools}, the analysis stage is a critical yet often fragmented part of the MD workflow. MDDash integrates three complementary analysis capabilities into a single interface, each addressing a different aspect of the analysis process.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Molecular visualization}: Mol*~\cite{molstar_viewer} is embedded directly in the dashboard, allowing users to inspect simulation structures and trajectories without leaving the platform. Crucially, this viewer is available not only after the simulation completes but also during an active run. This enables researchers to detect issues early and make informed decisions without waiting for the full simulation to finish.
    \item \textbf{MDDB workflow analyses}: The full suite of analyses provided by the MDDB Workflow~\cite{mddb_covid} is available for user-triggered execution. Results are displayed inline in the dashboard using visualization components shared with the MDRepo frontend~\cite{mdrepo_ui}, ensuring a consistent presentation of analysis outputs and reducing the overhead of learning a separate tool.
    \item \textbf{Custom analysis notebooks}: For analyses that fall outside the scope of the integrated tools, users can launch a Jupyter notebook environment from within the analyze step. This reuses the same notebook controller introduced in the Setup step, giving researchers unrestricted access to the full Python scientific stack and any GROMACS command-line tools. Users can write and execute arbitrary analysis scripts, inspect raw output files, or leverage the BioBB library, all within the same environment they used to prepare their simulation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Publish}
\label{sec:step_publish}

The final step allows researchers to publish their completed experiment directly to MDRepo. As discussed in Section~\ref{sec:reproducibility}, MDRepo combines the scalable InvenioRDM infrastructure of generalist repositories with domain-specific metadata schemas required for MD data, making it a natural integration target for MDDash.

The publish action is designed to require minimal user effort. MDDash automatically extracts simulation metadata using engine-specific tools and creates a pre-populated draft record on MDRepo, where the user can review it before finalizing. All experiment files, excluding temporary and cache artifacts, are uploaded alongside the metadata. A one-time OAuth authorization is required before the first publication.

This step also closes the reusability loop introduced in the new experiment step. Because MDRepo preserves both the simulation data and the associated Jupyter notebooks, any researcher can create a new MDDash experiment from a published record, retrieving input files and notebook workflows to reproduce or adapt the original experiment.

\section{User Interface Design}

JupyterHub handles user authentication and workspace isolation, while JupyterLab serves as the environment for notebook interaction. However, neither component provides a structured view of the simulation workflow or an overview of a user's experiments. A custom dashboard was therefore developed to serve as the main entry point, guiding users through the workflow steps and integrating access to JupyterLab, the MDRun service, and MDRepo under a single interface.

The dashboard is designed to be simple and intuitive, exposing only the information relevant to each workflow stage. The primary design goal was to create a seamless experience in which users can move between the dashboard, JupyterLab, and MDRepo without perceiving them as separate, disconnected tools.

\section{Tenant Isolation Strategy}
\label{sec:tenant_isolation}

MDDash operates within a two-tier namespace structure on a Kubernetes cluster managed by Rancher. The first tier is the \textit{administrative namespace}, which hosts the JupyterHub hub component, the MDRun API (Section~\ref{sec:mdrun_api}), Gromacs Tuner~\cite{gromacs_tuner}, and other shared services deployed as part of the Helm~\cite{helm} release. The second tier consists of \textit{user namespaces}, each dedicated to a single user. A user namespace is named according to the pattern \texttt{\{release\}-user-\{username\}-ns} and is provisioned dynamically upon the first server spawn of that user, as described in Section~\ref{sec:jupyterhub_orchestration}.

The namespace boundary enforces isolation at the Kubernetes resource level: workloads belonging to different users cannot directly access each other's pods, services, or storage volumes. Each user namespace is subject to a \texttt{ResourceQuota} that constrains the aggregate CPU and memory consumption of all pods running within it. Rather than managing these quotas through standalone Kubernetes manifests, MDDash delegates quota enforcement to Rancher through namespace-level annotations. Rancher reconciles these annotations by creating and maintaining the corresponding \texttt{ResourceQuota} object within the namespace, which avoids duplicating quota configuration across both the Helm chart and the provisioning logic. The quota values are configurable through Helm values and are propagated to the provisioning logic at runtime via environment variables.

Because the hub component resides in the administrative namespace, KubeSpawner requires explicit permission to manage resources across namespace boundaries. This is granted through a Kubernetes \texttt{Role} bound to the hub service account, scoped to the user namespace. A second role, bound to the user pod's own service account, permits the Dashboard API sidecar to submit and manage Kubernetes Jobs within the namespace on behalf of the user.

\section{Sidecar Container Pattern}
\label{sec:sidecar_pattern}

Each user pod is composed of multiple containers sharing the same network namespace and a common \texttt{PersistentVolumeClaim} (PVC) backed by an NFS storage class. The PVC uses \texttt{ReadWriteMany} access mode so that it can be mounted simultaneously by the user pod, Jupyter notebook pods, and analysis job pods --- all of which must access the same filesystem. The primary container runs the JupyterHub single-user notebook server. Three functional sidecar containers are attached to every user pod:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Proxy}: A Caddy-based reverse proxy that serves as the sole externally exposed endpoint of the pod. It routes incoming requests to the appropriate internal service based on the request path, enforces authentication through the \texttt{forward\_auth} mechanism described in Section~\ref{sec:security_architecture}, and serves the static frontend assets of the dashboard.
    \item \textbf{Authentication sidecar}: A lightweight Flask service responsible for validating session tokens against JupyterHub, as described in Section~\ref{sec:security_architecture}. It is consulted by the proxy on every incoming request before the request is forwarded to its target.
    \item \textbf{Dashboard API}: The Flask application implementing all business logic of the dashboard, including experiment management, job submission, and integration with external services. Its implementation is detailed in Section~\ref{sec:dashboard_api}.
\end{itemize}

An optional fourth sidecar is included when S3 object storage integration is configured. This container continuously synchronizes the contents of the user's S3 bucket with the shared PVC, making simulation outputs accessible to all other containers in the pod through the shared filesystem.

The proxy container delays accepting external traffic until the authentication sidecar and the Dashboard API are ready, preventing requests from reaching an incompletely initialized pod. All sidecar containers run under the hardened security context described in Section~\ref{sec:security_architecture}.

\section{JupyterHub Orchestration}
\label{sec:jupyterhub_orchestration}

MDDash is deployed as a Helm chart that wraps the upstream JupyterHub chart as a dependency, alongside the MDRun API and Gromacs Tuner sub-charts. Deployment-specific configuration is maintained in a template processed by Gomplate~\cite{gomplate} --- a general-purpose template renderer that supports external data sources --- rather than a plain values file. The template is rendered against an environment-specific configuration file before each deployment, producing the final Helm values. This separates the deployment topology from the chart logic and avoids duplicating values across environments.

The \textit{pre-spawn hook} is invoked by KubeSpawner before the user pod is created. It provisions the user namespace, resource quota, RBAC roles and bindings, and PVC described in Section~\ref{sec:tenant_isolation} if they do not already exist, making the first spawn of a user identical to any subsequent one from the hook's perspective.

The sidecar container specifications are not defined in Helm templates. Instead, the hub pod receives the sidecar image references as environment variables at deployment time. The pre-spawn hook reads these variables at spawn time to construct the container specifications and assigns them to the spawner's \texttt{extra\_containers} field. This design means that updating a sidecar image requires only a values change and a chart upgrade, with no modification to the spawning logic itself.

The \textit{post-stop hook} is invoked when the user's server is stopped. It zeroes the namespace's resource quota and deletes all running pods within the user namespace. This releases allocated cluster resources without destroying the namespace or the PVC, so that the user's data persists across sessions. The subsequent spawn restores the resource quota through the pre-spawn hook before any new pods are created.

The \textit{pod modification hook} runs after KubeSpawner has assembled the pod specification but before it is submitted to the Kubernetes API. It applies a hardened security context to the notebook container, enforcing the constraints described in Section~\ref{sec:security_architecture}.

\section{Dashboard API}
\label{sec:dashboard_api}

The Dashboard API is a Flask~\cite{flask} application that exposes endpoints for managing experiments, simulation jobs, analysis tasks, and notebook instances. It uses SQLAlchemy~\cite{sqlalchemy} for data persistence and Marshmallow~\cite{marshmallow} for serialization.

The SQLAlchemy models follow the Active Record pattern: each model class combines the database schema definition with domain logic, including lifecycle methods, computed properties, and status resolution. Because several model properties reflect runtime state from external systems --- such as the current phase of a Kubernetes Job or the progress of a running simulation --- these are computed on demand and cached with short time-to-live values to avoid excessive API calls. Integration with external services (Kubernetes, MDRun API, Gromacs Tuner, Caddy, MDRepo) is handled through dedicated client modules, which isolates the external communication logic from the domain models. Marshmallow auto-schemas are used to serialize both database columns and computed properties into a consistent response format for the frontend.

All persistent data is stored under a single directory, \texttt{DATA\_DIR}, which maps to the shared PVC described in Section~\ref{sec:tenant_isolation}. This includes the SQLite database, the Alembic migration history, and all experiment files such as simulation inputs and outputs. Using the PVC as the central data store means no separate database server is required, and all data survives pod restarts and deployments by default.

Database schema changes are managed through Flask-Migrate, which wraps the Alembic migration framework~\cite{alembic}. On each application startup, the API detects model changes, generates the corresponding migration, and applies all pending migrations. Because the migration files reside on the PVC alongside the database, no manual migration step is needed in the deployment pipeline.

\section{User Interface}
\label{sec:user_interface}

The dashboard frontend is a single-page application (SPA) built with React~\cite{reactjs}. Component styling is provided by shadcn/ui~\cite{shadcnui}, a library of accessible pre-styled primitives. Client-side navigation is handled by TanStack Router~\cite{tanstack_router} with a configurable base path, which is a prerequisite for the runtime configuration mechanism described below. Server state is managed by TanStack Query~\cite{tanstack_query}, which provides automatic background polling, caching, and request deduplication. Interactive 3D visualization of molecular structures and simulation trajectories is provided by the Mol* library~\cite{molstar_viewer}.

The compiled frontend assets are embedded in the Caddy proxy container image (Section~\ref{sec:caddy_proxy}) and served by Caddy alongside its other responsibilities. The dashboard is mounted at a per-user path prefix that is not known at image build time. Embedding this value into the compiled JavaScript bundle would require a separate build artefact for each user. To avoid this, the proxy injects configuration at runtime through a dynamically generated script. When the browser first loads the application, Caddy resolves the configuration values from environment variables and delivers the script to the browser. The frontend reads the injected values at module initialization time to resolve the mount path, the API endpoint, and the authenticated user's identity. A single compiled image can thus be deployed for any number of users without modification.

The five-step wizard workflow, whose steps are described in Section~\ref{sec:workflow_steps}, manages experiment progress through backend-authoritative state. The step index for each experiment is stored in the backend database, and the frontend polls the backend periodically to detect step transitions that originate server-side. This design ensures that the displayed step remains consistent with the experiment record on page refresh and across concurrent browser sessions.

\section{MDRun API}
\label{sec:mdrun_api}

The MDRun API starts and supervises production simulations. Like the Dashboard API (Section~\ref{sec:dashboard_api}), it uses a Flask-based web layer and SQLAlchemy for data handling. Its role is to launch production runs for the selected simulation engine, performing the actual molecular dynamics computation after the simulation inputs have been prepared.

The service is deployed in the administrative namespace rather than in user namespaces. This is necessary because user namespaces are intentionally resource-constrained, while the MDRun API must remain responsive when creating and supervising Kubernetes Jobs. In practice, the service acts as a control-plane component: it starts HPC jobs and monitors their state by using background daemon threads that periodically query the Kubernetes API.

Although all users access the same MDRun API instance, they cannot browse each other's jobs. Each job is identified by a UUID, which is practically impossible to guess, and the API deliberately does not expose a global job-listing endpoint. Responsibility for tracking job identifiers is therefore left to the higher application layers. The associated transfer of simulation outputs is described in Section~\ref{sec:s3_sync}.

\section{Caddy Proxy}
\label{sec:caddy_proxy}

The Caddy proxy~\cite{caddy} serves as the reverse proxy and API gateway for all services exposed from the user pod. It was selected because it combines efficient static file serving with first-class WebSocket support, which is required for JupyterLab, and a dynamic configuration API that makes it possible to register or remove routes without restarting the proxy. In practice, Caddy delivers the frontend assets, forwards API requests to the Dashboard API, and proxies traffic to the corresponding JupyterLab instance, thereby presenting the pod as a single coherent web application despite its internal multi-container structure.

All routes are protected using Caddy's \texttt{forward\_auth} directive, which delegates request authentication to a dedicated sidecar container before forwarding the request to its target. The authentication model is described in Section~\ref{sec:security_architecture}.


\section{Security Architecture}
\label{sec:security_architecture}

MDDash delegates the primary responsibility for user authentication to JupyterHub, which handles the full OpenID Connect (OIDC)~\cite{oidc} identity provider. The MDDash security model builds on top of this by enforcing authentication at the proxy layer through a lightweight Flask-based authentication container running as a sidecar in every user pod.

\subsection{Authentication Flow}

When an incoming request arrives at Caddy, the \texttt{forward\_auth} directive forwards it to the authentication container before routing it to the target service. The auth container inspects the request's session cookie and validates it against JupyterHub. If the session is valid, the request proceeds normally; if it is not, the auth container returns a redirect to the JupyterHub login page. To avoid making a request to JupyterHub on every incoming request, a successful validation result is cached in an in-process session store. The stored session state is signed using HMAC, which allows the auth container to verify the integrity of the state on subsequent requests without contacting JupyterHub again.

\subsection{Component Isolation}

All containers in every user pod run under a shared security context enforced by the Rancher cluster. Each container runs as a non-root user (UID~1000, GID~1000), with privilege escalation explicitly disabled, all Linux capabilities dropped, and the seccomp profile set to \texttt{RuntimeDefault}. This configuration is applied uniformly and cannot be overridden by individual workloads, as Rancher enforces it at the cluster level.

Beyond the runtime constraints, the threat model for MDDash acknowledges a deliberate design trade-off: the platform is intended for deployment by individual research teams on their own infrastructure, and its users are expected to be scientists rather than adversarial actors. Because each user's pod is isolated to their own Kubernetes namespace, any attempt to misuse the system would primarily affect only that user's own environment. This assumption does not weaken the runtime security controls already in place, but it does inform the decision to rely on JupyterHub's authentication model rather than implementing a bespoke identity management layer.

\section{S3 Synchronization}
\label{sec:s3_sync}

Because the MDRun API (Section~\ref{sec:mdrun_api}) executes simulation jobs in a dedicated Kubernetes namespace that does not share a persistent volume with the MDDash API pod, a common shared filesystem cannot be used for data exchange between the two components. S3-compatible object storage serves as the integration point: any service that can reach the S3 endpoint can read or write simulation data regardless of which namespace it runs in. The S3 endpoint is configurable at deployment time, making the solution independent of any particular cloud provider or institution.

Synchronization is implemented with \texttt{rclone}, which runs as a sidecar container alongside the relevant API container. On the MDDash API side, the daemon operates bidirectionally: it monitors the local working directory for changes using a continuous watch loop and propagates them to S3, while simultaneously reflecting any remote changes back to the local filesystem. This ensures that experiment files are durable and accessible even if the pod is restarted. On the MDRun API side, only a one-directional upload is required: the simulation job writes output files to local storage, and the sidecar continuously uploads them to S3 as they are produced. This allows MDDash to stream live simulation output to the user via the S3 bucket without requiring a direct connection between the two namespaces.

\section{Development Workflow}

Two complementary concerns shape the development workflow: reproducibility of the development environment across contributors, and the effective use of generative AI coding assistants throughout the implementation. The following subsections address each in turn.

\subsection{Unified Development Environment}

To reduce environment-specific failures during development, the \- project uses a custom Dev Container configuration for Visual Studio Code. The main benefit of this approach is that every developer works inside the same prepared environment, with the same operating system base image, command-line tools, language runtimes, editor extensions, and formatting rules. This eliminates a large class of \enquote{works on my machine} problems that would otherwise arise from differences in locally installed Python, Node.js, Kubernetes tooling, or editor configuration.

The container image is based on Ubuntu and includes the tools required for the full development workflow, most notably Python, Node.js, Docker, \texttt{kubectl}, Helm, \texttt{gomplate}, and \texttt{yq}. A post-creation script prepares a dedicated Python virtual environment, installs development dependencies for the backend, and installs the frontend JavaScript dependencies. The Dev Container configuration also pre-installs the Visual Studio Code extensions used by the project and enforces consistent formatting through Ruff for Python and Prettier for TypeScript and React files. As a result, new contributors can start working with a fully configured environment immediately, while the rest of the team benefits from consistent tooling and predictable behavior across development machines.

\subsection{AI-Assisted Implementation}

Generative AI coding assistants operate most effectively when provided with explicit, structured context about the system they are assisting with. In a multi-component codebase such as MDDash, where each component follows distinct conventions and cross-component interactions are non-trivial, relying solely on the source code to convey architectural intent leads to generated code that is technically correct in isolation but inconsistent with established project patterns. The project therefore maintains a hierarchy of context documents following the widely adopted \texttt{AGENTS.md} convention --- one at the repository root and one per component --- that describe each component's purpose, design patterns, key dependencies, data flow, and cross-cutting constraints. A parallel document in the repository's code review configuration provides the same architectural overview to AI tooling integrated at the code review stage of the development workflow.

The structure of these documents mirrors the component hierarchy of the codebase itself. When an AI agent addresses a task within a specific component, it reads the component-level document for local conventions and entry points. The root document provides system-wide orientation and makes cross-component constraints --- such as the authentication flow or the requirement for in-cluster Kubernetes configuration --- visible without repeating them in every component file. Because the conventions these documents encode are not derivable from the source code alone, they are versioned alongside the source to ensure they remain authoritative as the architecture evolves.

\section{DevOps and Configuration Management}

MDDash adopts a unified approach to build automation, configuration management, and continuous delivery. All deployment parameters are maintained in a single source of truth per environment. Build steps are fully scripted and the pipeline is automated from source commit to running cluster workload. The following subsections describe the build tooling and configuration strategy, and the CI/CD pipeline that executes them.

\subsection{Build Tooling and Configuration}

The build process is orchestrated through a hierarchy of Makefiles. The root Makefile reads the current Git branch to determine the target environment: \texttt{master} maps to production, all other branches to development. It then delegates to component-specific Makefiles for building and pushing container images and to the Helm Makefile for chart deployment. A full build-push-deploy cycle can be triggered with a single command.

Each environment has a dedicated YAML configuration file --- \texttt{config.yaml} for production and \texttt{config.dev.yaml} for development. These files serve as the single source of truth for all deployment parameters, including resource limits, storage class, OIDC endpoints, and image references. Both the Gomplate template described in Section~\ref{sec:jupyterhub_orchestration} and the Makefiles read from these files, so a parameter change in one place propagates to the entire deployment.

Container images are tagged differently per environment. In development, all images share a static \texttt{dev} tag that is overwritten on every build. In production, each build produces an immutable tag of the form \texttt{YYYYMMDD-<commit-sha>}. This tag embeds the deployment date and the source commit, which makes it possible to identify the exact build running on the cluster and to roll back to any prior version.

\subsection{CI/CD Pipeline}

The pipeline is implemented as a GitHub Actions workflow triggered on pushes to the \texttt{dev} and \texttt{master} branches. It proceeds through the following stages:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Test} --- three test suites (Dashboard API, authentication sidecar, MDRun API) run in parallel before any build begins.
    \item \textbf{Build} --- each build job independently detects whether files relevant to its component have changed; unchanged components are skipped. In production, skipped builds are handled by re-tagging the most recently built image with the new deployment tag, ensuring all components carry a consistent tag set.
    \item \textbf{Deploy} --- triggered only when at least one component or the Helm chart has changed. The step provisions required Kubernetes secrets from CI-stored credentials and performs a Helm install or upgrade.
    \item \textbf{Verify} --- after deployment, the pipeline polls the JupyterHub health endpoint until the service responds successfully or a timeout is reached.
\end{enumerate}

All credentials required for deployment are stored as GitHub Actions secrets and provisioned as Kubernetes secrets during the deploy step.

\chapter{Evaluation}

The evaluation of MDDash proceeded along two complementary directions. First, the platform was exposed to domain researchers in order to obtain qualitative feedback on its suitability for real scientific work. Second, a comparative experiment was carried out to quantify the time required to complete a representative simulation across three approaches: the traditional command-line workflow described in Section~\ref{sec:md_workflow}, the BioExcel Building Blocks (BioBB) library~\cite{BioBB}, and MDDash.

\section{User Testing}
\label{sec:user_testing}

To obtain qualitative feedback on the platform, MDDash was presented to two researchers active in molecular dynamics: one from the Central European Institute of Technology (CEITEC) at Masaryk University, and one from the Center for Magnetic Resonance (CERM) at the University of Florence. Both researchers conduct production simulations as part of their routine work, with individual runs that can extend over several weeks to months of wall-clock time. This duration reinforces the importance of the tune step described in Section~\ref{sec:step_tune}: a configuration that is even a few percent below optimal translates, over a month-long simulation, into days of wasted compute.

One of the two researchers maintained a personal cheat sheet of GROMACS commands in a Word document. This practice illustrates the gap noted in Section~\ref{sec:md_software}: in the absence of a standardized interface, users curate their own ad hoc references, which are neither version-controlled nor shareable. The pre-configured Jupyter notebooks distributed with MDDash (Section~\ref{sec:step_setup}) encode the same procedural steps in an executable and self-documenting form, removing the need for such external references and reducing the risk of copy-paste errors across projects.

The feedback collected during these sessions led to two concrete changes. First, the experiment creation form originally restricted file uploads to a fixed set of pre-defined types; testers required the ability to upload arbitrary files to accommodate non-standard input formats, and the restriction was removed. Second, one researcher identified a race condition in the JupyterHub pre-spawn hook that could corrupt namespace provisioning when multiple spawns occurred in rapid succession; this required a substantial fix to the hook logic to serialize concurrent provisioning operations correctly.

Both researchers expressed a positive overall assessment, stating that they recognized the potential of MDDash and indicated willingness to adopt it once the platform is ready for production use. They noted that the primary remaining blocker is the dependency on MDRepo reaching production readiness, which prevents the publish step from being exercised end-to-end (Section~\ref{sec:step_publish}).

A broader and more systematic user study remains a direction for future work once this dependency is resolved.

\subsection{Limitations}

Across the three approaches, the active time required to complete the workflow end-to-end decreased from 271 minutes (manual) to 207 minutes (BioBB) to 76 minutes (MDDash), corresponding to reductions of 24 \% and 72 \% relative to the manual baseline, respectively. Several limitations bound the scope of these results.

The experiment was conducted by the thesis author, who is also the developer of MDDash. This introduces a systematic bias: the MDDash workflow was executed with complete familiarity with the interface, while the manual and BioBB workflows represent less practiced paths. Additionally, the author's GROMACS experience derives primarily from tool development rather than scientific use. A domain practitioner would complete the manual steps faster, particularly MDP authoring and analysis.

The reference system is a standard benchmark case with a well-documented preparation procedure. More complex systems (protein--ligand complexes, membrane proteins, or multi-component assemblies) require additional preparation steps and force field decisions absent here. The reported savings may therefore represent a lower bound.

The reference system also does not expose the file-handling overhead typical of production use. Runs lasting weeks on larger systems routinely produce tens to hundreds of gigabytes, requiring resumable transfer tools and often forcing analysis onto the HPC cluster directly. MDDash avoids this through continuous S3 synchronization (Section~\ref{sec:s3_sync}), but this advantage is absent from the current comparison.

The manual timing assumes systematic performance tuning is performed. In practice, researchers frequently skip this step. For such users, MDDash provides not a reduction in active work but an enabling of tuning that would not otherwise occur. The benefit is a faster production run rather than reduced setup effort, though the throughput improvement was not measured here.

Although the manual timings already reflect LLM-assisted work, LLMs partially overlap with MDDash's accessibility goal by reducing command-lookup and MDP authoring friction. They do not, however, address MDDash's remaining differentiators: automated performance tuning, provenance capture, reproducible notebooks, and integrated publication. The value of MDDash in an LLM-equipped environment therefore lies primarily in workflow integration and reproducibility.

Finally, the reported totals exclude the production run itself, which consumes identical compute time across all approaches. The advantage of MDDash therefore grows with simulation length: a sub-optimal hyperparameter configuration translates, over a month-long run, into days of additional wall-clock time, consistent with the observations in Section~\ref{sec:user_testing}.


\chapter{Conclusion}

This thesis presented MDDash, a virtual research environment that unifies the molecular dynamics simulation workflow within a single web-based platform. The system directly addresses the four limitations identified in Section~\ref{sec:identified_gaps}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Integration with the GROMACS Tuner service provides the systematic performance-tuning step absent from the standard workflow.
    \item A structured wizard interface built on top of JupyterHub and JupyterLab lowers the command-line entry barrier imposed by existing simulation engines.
    \item Direct integration with MDRepo and its domain-specific metadata schema replaces the fragmented data stewardship typical of generalist repositories.
    \item Embedding Mol*, the MDDB workflow analyses, and interactive notebook sessions into the dashboard unifies analysis with the surrounding simulation lifecycle.
\end{itemize}

MDDash is fully operational and is deployed on the e-INFRA~CZ Kubernetes infrastructure across separate production and development environments. Its production instance is publicly accessible at \url{https://mddash.dyn.cloud.e-infra.cz}, and its complete source code is maintained as an open-source project at \url{https://github.com/CERIT-SC/mddash}.

At present, the platform has no active end users. Its publication workflow depends on MDRepo reaching production readiness, which had not occurred at the time of writing. The final step of the workflow therefore cannot yet be exercised end-to-end against a live target.

Several directions are identified for future work. Completing the MDRepo integration once it reaches production will close the reusability loop described in Section~\ref{sec:step_new_experiment}. A UI/UX rework, already underway, aims to make the wizard more descriptive and intuitive for researchers without prior exposure to the platform. Recruiting external users will be essential for validating the design choices in practice and for directing subsequent refinements. Finally, the modular engine architecture described in Section~\ref{sec:md_software} is intended to support additional simulation engines beyond the currently supported GROMACS and AMBER.

\section*{Personal reflection}

Developing MDDash over the course of a year provided an opportunity to work across a wide range of technologies, from Kubernetes and Helm chart authoring to React frontend development. It also gave me familiarity with a scientific domain that lay outside my prior experience. The project was built from scratch against a minimal set of functional requirements agreed upon with the team, and it evolved continuously in response to later discussions and practical constraints.

A guiding principle throughout was to keep a working version deployed in production at all times. This made it possible to demonstrate the current state of the system at any moment, and it steadily shaped the architecture toward operational robustness and incremental delivery.

Because MDDash is an open-source project, particular emphasis was placed on code maintainability and readability. During the project I also integrated generative AI assistants into my development workflow, which in turn motivated the context-document hierarchy described earlier in Section~\ref{sec:devops_configuration}. The public repository reflects the cumulative effort invested in the system over this period and is intended to remain a foundation for continued development beyond the scope of this thesis.
