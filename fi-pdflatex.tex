The application of molecular dynamics (MD) has evolved significantly since its inception in the 1950s~\cite{md_for_all}. The subsequent rise of computational power has made it highly accessible and practical. Its adoption accelerated during the COVID-19 pandemic, where it played a crucial role in the development of vaccines~\cite{md_in_covid}.

A primary challenge current MD scientists face is the lack of standardization in setting up, tuning, running, and sharing simulations. The most popular MD simulation software, GROMACS, relies on a command-line interface, meaning users frequently depend on custom scripts and manual configuration. MD simulations are computationally expensive, necessitating the correct selection of hyperparameters, such as the number of MPI processes or the allocation of CPU and GPU resources. Determining these hyperparameters is complex, and sub-optimal configurations lead to significant computational overhead and resource inefficiency. Upon obtaining the results, researchers require separate sets of tools for analysis, further increasing workflow overhead. After completing this complex process, reusing the results is difficult due to the absence of a standardized repository for sharing molecular dynamics data; data exchange consequently relies on custom shell scripts and non-standard methodologies. This environment creates a barrier between teams and hinders collaboration.

MD~Dash is a tool created to address these challenges. It aims to provide scientists with an efficient workflow that integrates simulation, analysis, and data management. It provides an easy to use web interface for setting up, running and analyzing molecular dynamics simulations using GROMACS. It also includes an automatic hyperparameter tuner that optimizes the simulation parameters based on the user's hardware and simulation system. To support reproducibility and data preservation, MD~Dash utilizes reusable Jupyter notebooks that record all simulation steps, implements automatic metadata extraction using the Gromacs Metadump tool, and integrates directly with the InvenioRDM repository for publishing simulation results. These features ensure that simulation data is well-documented, standardized, and easily accessible for future use.


\chapter{Molecular Dynamics landscape}
\label{ch:md_landscape}

MD simulations are a powerful computational technique used to model the physical movements of atoms and molecules over time. They provide insights into the structure, dynamics, and interactions of biological macromolecules, which are crucial for drug discovery and understanding fundamental biological processes~\cite{md_for_all}. The primary benefits of MD lie in its ability to provide atomic-level resolution, capturing details that are often inaccessible through experimental methods such as X-ray crystallography or NMR spectroscopy. By enabling the observation of rare events and the calculation of thermodynamic properties like binding free energies, MD simulations serve as a bridge between theoretical models and experimental observations. This computational approach reduces the costs and time associated with traditional wet-lab experiments by allowing researchers to pre-screen candidates and simulate complex biological environments with high precision.

Setting up and running MD simulations is a complex process that involves multiple steps, each with its own set of parameters and configurations. This complexity can be a barrier for researchers, especially those who are new to the field or do not have extensive computational expertise.

\section{Typical MD Workflow}
\label{sec:md_workflow}

When conducting MD research, scientists typically follow this workflow:~\cite{gmx_workflow}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Topology generation}: generating force field-compliant topology files from the initial structure, including hydrogen atom addition and force field selection.
    \item \textbf{Box definition and solvent addition}: defining the simulation box dimensions with appropriate buffer distance and filling the box with solvent molecules.
    \item \textbf{Ion addition}: neutralizing the system by adding counter-ions to balance the net charge, and optionally adding ions to achieve desired ionic strength.
    \item \textbf{Energy minimization}: relaxing the system through energy minimization to remove steric clashes, inappropriate geometry, and bad contacts.
    \item \textbf{Equilibration}: equilibrating the system in two phases: first under NVT ensemble (constant number, volume, and temperature) to stabilize temperature, then under NPT ensemble (constant number, pressure, and temperature) to stabilize pressure and density, bringing the system to proper experimental conditions.
    \item \textbf{Production MD}: running the actual molecular dynamics simulation for data collection with position restraints released.
    \item \textbf{Analysis}: post-processing trajectories to extract meaningful insights, including structural properties, energetics, and dynamic behavior.
    \item \textbf{Publication}: sharing the simulation data and results with the scientific community, often through repositories or supplementary materials in publications.
\end{enumerate}

\section{MD Simulation Software}
\label{sec:md_software}

For running molecular dynamics simulations, there are several software packages available, each with its own strengths and weaknesses. Some of the most common molecular simulation engines include GROMACS, AMBER, LAMMPS, and CHARMM~\cite{gmx_software_comparison}. While these programs are designed to perform similar tasks and evaluate the same underlying physical models, they often have different default parameters, integration schemes, and even variations in fundamental constants (such as Coulomb's constant). These differences can lead to variations in simulation results and make cross-engine reproducibility challenging if not carefully managed~\cite{gmx_software_comparison}.

Historically, GROMACS has been one of the most established and widely used MD engines due to its high performance, extensive features, and active community. However, recent trends indicate that while both GROMACS and LAMMPS have seen a surge in popularity since mid-2025, LAMMPS has surpassed GROMACS in overall interest~\cite{gmx_trend}. Despite its popularity, GROMACS is primarily designed as a command-line tool, which can make it less accessible to users who are not comfortable with terminal-based workflows. This often leads to reliance on custom scripts and manual setup processes, which can be error-prone and difficult to reproduce. While LAMMPS is also fundamentally a command-line program, it benefits from a third-party GUI~\cite{lammps_tutorials_2025} that can simplify accessibility for less experienced researchers.

\section{Reproducibility and Data Management}
\label{sec:reproducibility}

The \enquote{reproducibility crisis} in computational molecular science stems from several factors, primarily the lack of a standardized, centralized repository for sharing molecular dynamics data. Modern MD simulations generate massive volumes of trajectory data, creating a \enquote{data deluge}. Historically, these outputs were hoarded on local hard drives or institutional servers, often discarded after publication. Driven by Open Science mandates and the FAIR (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable) principles, there is now a strong demand for robust data stewardship~\cite{md_reproducibility}.

In the absence of a unified global infrastructure, researchers frequently rely on generalist data repositories such as Zenodo, Figshare and Open Science Framework (OSF)~\cite{md_repo_problem}. Zenodo, in particular, is widely used and is built upon the InvenioRDM framework -- a turn-key research data management platform developed by CERN. InvenioRDM is designed to handle large-scale research data, providing long-term preservation and assigning persistent identifiers like Digital Object Identifiers (DOIs). While generalist repositories provide essential infrastructure, their agnostic nature allows the deposition of arbitrary file types without enforcing domain-specific metadata standards. This practice has led to the accumulation of \enquote{dark matter}~\cite{md_repo_problem} -- petabytes of trajectory files that lack the necessary contextual metadata (such as force field parameters, integration temperatures, or system topologies) required for independent verification or automated meta-analysis.

To address the limitations of generalist platforms, the biomolecular community has developed domain-specific repositories, such as GPCRmd or MoDEL~\cite{md_repo_problem}. These specialized platforms enforce strict metadata schemas and integrate web-based visualization tools. However, this specialization introduces architectural fragmentation, as independent databases often utilize disparate ontologies and APIs, hindering cross-domain interoperability.

Bridging the gap between robust generalist infrastructure and domain-specific requirements is essential for the future of MD data sharing. Platforms like MDRepo address this architectural challenge. MDRepo is built on top of the same InvenioRDM framework that powers Zenodo, inheriting its scalable architecture and persistent identifier management. However, unlike Zenodo, MDRepo is explicitly tailored for molecular dynamics, enforcing rigorous metadata schemas and standardizing trajectory formats. By integrating such a repository directly into the simulation workflow, researchers can ensure that their data is not only preserved but remains fully interpretable and reusable for future computational research.

\section{Virtual Research Environments}
\label{sec:VREs}

To address the complexities of MD orchestration and data management, the scientific community has developed Virtual Research Environments (VREs). These comprehensive, web-based platforms provide researchers with secure access to distributed HPC tools, federated data repositories, and collaborative services. A prominent example is the BioExcel Center of Excellence and its BioExcel Building Blocks (BioBB) library~\cite{hpc_approaches_for_md}, which encapsulates complex biomolecular simulation tools into interoperable Python wrappers distributed as Jupyter Notebooks -- allowing researchers to execute, document, and share entire simulation pipelines within a single readable document. Another example is the WeNMR project, which utilizes the DIRAC workload manager to execute millions of jobs across global grid infrastructures~\cite{vre_for_biology}.

Despite these advancements, several challenges remain. The \enquote{Exabyte problem} highlights the logistical difficulty of moving petabytes of raw trajectory data across networks for analysis. Consequently, modern VREs must adopt a \enquote{data gravitation} approach, bringing analytical tools directly to the centralized data stores. While tools like BioBB lower the barrier to entry through Jupyter Notebooks, many existing VREs still require significant expertise in command-line interfaces, complex scripting, and manual infrastructure management, maintaining a steep learning curve for experimental researchers.

\section{Cloud-Native Technologies in Science}

Scientific computing is increasingly adopting cloud-native technologies to address the challenges of scalability, reproducibility, and collaboration. Kubernetes has emerged as a leading platform for orchestrating containerized applications, providing features like resource management, scalability, and isolation that are essential for scientific workloads.

JupyterHub provides an interactive coding environment that is familiar to many researchers, allowing them to write and execute code in a web-based interface while leveraging the underlying computational resources managed by Kubernetes. These technologies enable researchers to focus on their scientific questions rather than the complexities of managing infrastructure, making them ideal for building modern virtual research environments.

\chapter{Analysis and Design}

MD~Dash was designed to address the challenges identified in Chapter~\ref{ch:md_landscape}. Its primary objectives include providing a user-friendly interface to abstract simulation complexities, automating parameter optimization, and ensuring reproducibility through Jupyter notebook and InvenioRDM integration.

\section{Workflow design}

Following the workflow structure described in Section~\ref{sec:md_workflow}, MD~Dash was designed to guide users through all necessary steps of their molecular dynamics simulations. The workflow is structured as a linear wizard with six distinct steps:

\begin{enumerate}
    \setcounter{enumi}{-1}
    \item \textbf{New experiment}
    \item \textbf{Setup}
    \item \textbf{Tune}
    \item \textbf{Run}
    \item \textbf{Analyze}
    \item \textbf{Publish}
\end{enumerate}

These steps are designed to provide a clear and structured path for users, ensuring that they do not miss any critical components of the simulation process. By breaking down the workflow into discrete stages, MD~Dash promotes reproducibility by enforcing a standardized sequence of actions and capturing all necessary metadata at each step. This contrasts with the traditional unstructured terminal approach, where users may skip steps or fail to document their process, leading to difficulties in reproducing results.

\section{Platform choice}

To focus on integrating the workflow rather than building the application from scratch, it was decided to leverage the capabilities of JupyterHub for user management and resource allocation. As discussed in section~\ref{sec:VREs}, some researchers are already familiar with the Jupyter ecosystem. Most biochemistry scientists are also familiar with Python and its scientific libraries, so it was a natural choice to use Jupyter notebooks as the main interface for setting up and analyzing simulations. JupyterHub provides a multi-user environment that allows each user to have their own isolated workspace, which is essential for running multiple computations concurrently without interference. Additionally, JupyterHub can be easily deployed on Kubernetes, which provides robust resource management and scalability options.

\section{Workflow steps}

\subsection{New experiment}

The initialization of a new experiment is designed with a focus on reproducibility and workflow flexibility. To facilitate these principles, MD~Dash supports several methods for retrieving starting structures and simulation parameters. Users can instantiate an experiment directly from the MDRepo repository, enabling the reuse of verified, previously published simulations as templates. Alternatively, structural data can be fetched by providing a PDB ID from the RCSB Protein Data Bank (PDB) or by uploading local configuration files directly.

Furthermore, the environment allows users to specify a remote Git repository for the Jupyter notebooks used in subsequent setup and analysis stages. This feature includes support for Binder-compatible repositories, which provides native compatibility with the BioExcel Building Blocks (BioBB) library discussed in section~\ref{sec:VREs}. By integrating these diverse input sources, the platform ensures that researchers adhere to standardized protocols and also have the flexibility to customize their workflows according to their specific research needs.

\subsection{Setup}

Traditionally, setting up MD simulations involves a series of manual commands executed in a terminal, often documented in a text file or a custom script. This approach can lead to issues with reproducibility, as it relies heavily on the user's diligence in recording every step and parameter used. In contrast, using reusable Jupyter notebooks for the setup process offers several advantages:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Documentation and Reproducibility}: Jupyter notebooks inherently combine code, documentation, and results in a single document. This allows researchers to capture the entire setup process, including the rationale behind each step, in a format that is easy to understand and reproduce.
    \item \textbf{Flexibility and Customization}: Notebooks can be easily modified and extended. Researchers can add new cells for additional setup steps, modify existing code to accommodate specific requirements, or integrate new tools without needing to rewrite an entire script.
    \item \textbf{Interactive Environment}: Jupyter notebooks provide an interactive environment where researchers can execute code cells individually, allowing for iterative development and immediate feedback. This is particularly beneficial during the setup phase, where adjustments may be necessary based on intermediate results.
    \item \textbf{Integration with BioBB}: By supporting Binder-compatible repositories, the platform allows users to leverage the BioExcel Building Blocks library, which provides pre-built workflows for biomolecular simulations. This integration further enhances the ease of use and standardization of the setup process.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Tune}

Correct parameter selection for the simulation is often omitted from the traditional MD workflow~(\ref{sec:md_workflow}). It is however the most important step for efficient use of computational resources. If the parameters are not selected correctly, the process can run much slower than it should, which can lead to increased costs and longer time to results. The tune step in MD~Dash addresses this issue by providing an automated way to optimize the hyperparameters. This is achieved through integration with a GROMACS Tuner~\cite{gromacs_tuner} service, which performs short benchmark runs with different configurations and selects the best one based on performance metrics. By automating this process, MD~Dash ensures that users can achieve optimal performance without needing to manually experiment with different settings, thus saving time and computational resources.

\subsection{Run}

After obtaining the optimal parameters from the tuning step, users can proceed to run their simulations directly from the MD~Dash interface. The platform utilizes Kubernetes Jobs to manage the execution of GROMACS simulations, providing correct resource allocation and isolation for each simulation run. The MDRun API (\ref{sec:mdrun_api}) serves as the interface for starting and monitoring these simulations, allowing users to track the progress and resource usage in real-time. Additionally, MD~Dash provides live file access during the simulation run, enabling users to inspect intermediate results and make informed decisions about their simulations without needing to wait for completion.

\section{User Interface Design}

JupyterHub provides a simple and flexible interface for handling user authentication and isolation. JupyterLab serves as the main environment for notebook interaction. However to tie everything together a custom dashboard was required to guide users through the workflow and provide an overview of their experiments. The dashboard also serves as the main entry point for accessing the JupyterLab environment and all other external services. It is designed to be simple and intuitive, with a focus on providing users with the information they need to manage their experiments effectively. The goal was to create a seamless experience where users can easily navigate between the dashboard, JupyterHub, JupyterLab and MDRepo without feeling like they are using multiple disconnected tools.
